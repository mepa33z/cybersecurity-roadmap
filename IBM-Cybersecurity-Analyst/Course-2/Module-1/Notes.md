This course item explains the CIA Triad, a foundational concept in cybersecurity that emphasizes Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability as essential components of a comprehensive security program.

Confidentiality:

Ensures data is protected from unauthorized access to prevent identity theft, compromised accounts, legal issues, and reputational damage.
Achieved by controlling data access and using security tools like encryption and multifactor authentication (MFA).

Integrity:

Guarantees data accuracy, completeness, and consistency during storage, processing, and transit.
Maintained through physical safeguards, logical checks, file integrity monitoring, and relational database management systems (RDBMS) to prevent unauthorized changes.

Availability:

Ensures authorized users have reliable and immediate access to data when needed.
Protected by network monitoring, redundant hardware, backup servers, and defenses against attacks like Denial of Service (DoS).

Regulatory Standards and Penalties:

Laws such as HIPAA (for health information in the U.S.) and GDPR (for digital privacy in the EU) regulate data security and privacy.
Non-compliance with these regulations can result in significant fines and penalties, encouraging organizations to maintain strong data protection practices.


This course item focuses on the concepts of information security, intellectual property, and the handling of confidential information in a business context.

Information Assets and Data Analytics:

Information assets are valuable data or information, existing physically or electronically, such as patient records or intellectual property.
Data analytics transforms raw data (facts and values) into meaningful information and insights that support business decisions.

Intellectual Property and Digital Products:

Intellectual property (IP) includes creations of the mind like designs, trade secrets, and research discoveries, protected by laws such as copyright and patents.
Digital products are intangible assets like software, eBooks, and web elements, which require protection from piracy and hacking using tools like Digital Rights Management (DRM).

Data-Driven Business Decisions and Data Handling:

Data-driven decisions rely on capturing, correlating, and reporting data from various sources to identify trends and improve business processes.
Proper handling of confidential information involves restricting access, secure storage, destruction of unnecessary files, and employee training to protect Personally Identifiable Information (PII), company, customer, and health information.

Confidential Information Types and Security Practices:

Four main types of confidential information are PII, company confidential, customer confidential, and protected health information (PHI).
Security practices include encryption, firewalls, strong passwords, NDAs, and compliance with laws like the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) to prevent unauthorized access and data breaches.


Information privacy means protecting people’s personal information from being used, shared, stolen, or accessed without permission. It applies to things like names, addresses, health records, financial details, online activity, and account information.

HIPAA is a U.S. privacy law focused on healthcare information. It protects “protected health information,” also called PHI, such as medical records, diagnoses, insurance details, and treatment information. HIPAA applies to healthcare providers, health plans, and their business partners. These organizations must keep patient information private, secure it properly, and give patients rights over their health records.

GDPR is the European Union’s major privacy law. It protects personal data for people in the EU and gives individuals rights such as knowing how their data is used, requesting deletion, correcting data, and limiting how companies process it. GDPR can also apply to companies outside Europe if they handle data from people in the EU.

Different countries apply privacy differently. The U.S. uses several sector-based laws, like HIPAA for healthcare, FERPA for education, and FCRA for credit reporting. The EU uses GDPR as a broad privacy law across many industries. Other countries have created similar privacy laws, and many companies must follow multiple rules depending on where their users or customers live.

Companies apply information privacy by limiting who can access data, encrypting information, asking for consent, writing privacy policies, training employees, reporting data breaches, and only collecting the data they truly need. A company like a hospital may focus heavily on HIPAA, while a social media or tech company with European users may need to follow GDPR too.

Simple takeaway: HIPAA protects health privacy in the U.S.; GDPR protects personal data broadly in Europe; and information privacy is about making sure personal data is collected, used, stored, and shared responsibly.


This course item explains various types of security threats and how to protect hardware and data from them.

Physical and Environmental Threats:

Physical security involves restricting access to hardware to authorized personnel and securing devices in locked areas with surveillance.
Environmental risks include power outages, fires, natural disasters, humidity, and mold, which can damage hardware; mitigation includes fire suppression, backup power, and HVAC systems.

Data Threats and Insider Risks:

Data leaks happen accidentally, while data breaches are intentional exposures caused by cybercriminals using social engineering or phishing.
Insider threats come from employees who may intentionally or accidentally compromise data, sometimes influenced by hackers.

Software Threats and Malware:

Software threats include theft, exploits, and malware, which can come from infected websites, attachments, or devices.
Malware types include viruses, worms, trojans, spyware, adware, and ransomware, with polymorphic viruses being the most common form used to evade detection.


To protect hardware from physical and environmental threats, you can:

Restrict physical access to authorized personnel only, using locked secured areas with card readers.
Use robust surveillance systems inside and outside the premises, keeping them maintained and tested.
Protect against environmental risks by installing fire suppression systems, backup power supplies, and properly functioning HVAC systems to control humidity and prevent mold.
Develop and regularly update a disaster recovery plan, including mock drills to prepare for incidents like power outages, fires, or natural disasters.


Designing a disaster recovery plan for hardware protection involves these key steps:

Risk Assessment: Identify potential physical and environmental threats (e.g., fire, flood, power outage).

Preventive Measures: Implement safeguards like fire suppression, backup power, HVAC systems, and secure access controls.

Backup Strategy: Regularly back up critical data and system configurations to offsite or cloud storage.

Response Procedures: Define clear steps for staff to follow during and after a disaster, including communication protocols.

Recovery Steps: Outline how to restore hardware and systems from backups and verify their integrity.

Testing and Training: Conduct regular drills and update the plan based on lessons learned to ensure readiness.


Threat Types:

This course item explains different types of cybersecurity threats, focusing on how attackers intercept data and disrupt networks.

Snooping Attacks:

Snooping involves intercepting data between devices, revealing sensitive information like logins and credit card numbers.
Types include eavesdropping (packet-sniffing), man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks, and replay attacks, often occurring on unsecured networks.

Man-in-the-Middle and Replay Attacks:

MITM attacks involve an attacker secretly intercepting communication between a victim and a recipient, either physically or logically.
Replay attacks capture and retransmit data using stolen access tokens to impersonate trusted entities and hijack sessions.

Cross-Site Scripting and SQL Injection:

Cross-site scripting (XSS) injects malicious code into trusted websites, either via URLs or comment sections, to steal user data.
SQL injection uses malicious SQL code to bypass website security, allowing attackers to access or delete database information.

Botnets and Denial of Service Attacks:

Botnets are networks of malware-infected computers controlled remotely to perform large-scale attacks or resource-intensive tasks like cryptomining.
Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks overwhelm networks with traffic to cause crashes, with DDoS using botnets for greater impact.


This course item explains impersonation attacks, social engineering, and related cyber threats, along with ways to minimize exposure to them.

Types of Impersonation Attacks

Public Wi-Fi traps ("Evil Twin") intercept user credentials by mimicking legitimate networks.
Fake websites replicate real ones to steal login information.
Social engineering involves pretending to be someone else to steal data or control systems.

Social Engineering Techniques

Shoulder surfing: spying on screens or keyboards in public places to capture sensitive data.
Baiting: using physical or digital lures (e.g., USB sticks, online ads) to infect systems with malware.
Pretexting: impersonating authority figures to pressure victims into revealing confidential information.
Phishing: sending urgent emails or texts to trick victims into revealing credentials or installing malware.
Consequences and Prevention

Social engineering contributes to about 90% of security breaches.
Stolen information can lead to identity theft and fraud.
To reduce risk, avoid public Wi-Fi, never leave devices unattended, use privacy screens, keep personal info secure, use strong passwords, and monitor financial accounts regularly.


This course content focuses on physical security as a critical component of overall cybersecurity, emphasizing protection of devices and infrastructure from physical threats and environmental hazards.

Physical Security Fundamentals

Physical security involves measures to protect computing devices and network infrastructure from theft, damage, or unauthorized access.
It is essential because physical access can bypass many software-based security controls, making it the first line of defense.
Key Tools and Best Practices

Cable locks and USB locks help prevent device theft and unauthorized data access.
Secure storage solutions and access control systems (e.g., key cards, biometrics) restrict entry to sensitive areas.

Environmental and Access Controls

Environmental protections include surge protectors, UPS, climate control, fire suppression, and water damage prevention.
Proper workstation placement, screen privacy, mobile device security, and visitor management enhance physical security.

Incident Response and Integration

Incident response plans cover reporting, securing areas, and recovery steps like data restoration.
Physical security should integrate with cybersecurity measures such as encryption and network access controls for comprehensive protection.


This course content describes notable cybersecurity breaches and attack methods targeting major companies.

Yahoo and Phishing Attacks:

Yahoo suffered two major breaches by state-sponsored actors between 2013 and 2014, compromising billions of user records including personal details and security questions.
A phishing attack in 2014 involved an employee clicking a malicious link, leading to the theft of 500 million account details, which were later sold on the black market.

Equifax and Web Application Vulnerability:

In 2017, Equifax experienced a breach due to failure to apply a critical software patch, exposing sensitive data of 148 million people.
The breach went undetected for months, resulting in significant financial losses and reputational damage.

Court Ventures and Social Engineering:

Court Ventures, an Experian subsidiary, was compromised through social engineering by an impersonator who accessed confidential data.
The attacker sold social security and credit card numbers of 200 million US residents before being sentenced.

T-Mobile and Brute Force Attacks:

In 2021, T-Mobile was breached via brute force attacks, exposing personal and device information of over 50 million customers.
The company faced a $500 million lawsuit and committed substantial funds to improve security.
These cases illustrate common cybersecurity threats such as phishing, social engineering, unpatched vulnerabilities, and brute force attacks, highlighting the importance of robust security practices.
