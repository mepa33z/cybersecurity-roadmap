This course item explains the concept of threat actors in cybersecurity, categorizing them by their motives and highlighting the importance of understanding these actors to develop effective cybersecurity strategies.

Definition and Motives of Threat Actors:

A threat actor is an individual or group responsible for harmful events targeting computers, networks, or systems.
Their motives include financial gain, political agendas, espionage, or causing disruption and chaos.

Types of Threat Actors:

Hacktivists: Driven by political or social causes, using attacks like website defacement or denial of service to promote their views (e.g., Anonymous).

Organized Crime Syndicates: Sophisticated groups focused on financial crimes such as ransomware and identity theft (e.g., Lazarus group).

Nation State Actors: Government-sponsored actors conducting espionage or cyberattacks to advance geopolitical interests, often highly capable (e.g., cyber warfare in Russia-Ukraine conflict).

Script Kiddies: Inexperienced individuals using existing tools for hacking, often without clear motives but sometimes causing significant damage (e.g., Mafiaboy attack).

Insider Threat Actors: Individuals within organizations causing harm either intentionally or unintentionally, such as through leaks or negligence (e.g., Edward Snowden case).

Importance of Studying Threat Actors:

Each type of threat actor presents unique challenges requiring tailored cybersecurity responses.
Understanding the diverse landscape of threat actors is critical for formulating effective cybersecurity defenses and managing evolving risks.




This course content explains the concept of insider attacks in cybersecurity, focusing on the different types of threats posed by insiders within an organization.

Types of Insider Attacks:

Insider attacks are carried out by individuals with authorized access to systems and data, and can be either intentional or unintentional.
These attacks are particularly dangerous because they originate from within the organization.

Categories of Insider Threats:

Oblivious Insider: An employee who unintentionally causes security risks due to lack of awareness, such as falling for phishing scams.

Negligent Insider: Someone who disregards security protocols or bypasses policies, potentially exposing systems or sensitive information.

Malicious and Professional Insiders:

Malicious Insider: A knowledgeable individual who intentionally harms the organization for reasons like personal grievance or financial gain.

Professional Insider: A person recruited by espionage groups to steal intellectual property or confidential information for competitors or foreign entities.



The course content focuses on the future of cybersecurity, emphasizing the increasing role of AI in both threats and defenses.

Future Trends in Authentication and Threats:

The shift from passwords to passkeys using the Fido standard aims to improve security and usability by eliminating password sharing vulnerabilities.
AI-driven threats such as sophisticated phishing emails and deep fakes will become more prevalent, requiring new security approaches beyond traditional detection.

Challenges with AI and Generative Technologies:

Deep fake technology is advancing rapidly, making detection difficult; security strategies must focus on mitigating risks rather than relying solely on detection.
Generative AI can produce inaccurate information ("hallucinations"), posing risks when used for decision-making in security contexts; improvements like retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) are expected to enhance accuracy.

Symbiotic Relationship Between AI and Cybersecurity:

AI will be leveraged to improve cybersecurity defenses by anticipating attacks and analyzing threats more effectively.
Cybersecurity expertise will be essential to secure AI systems themselves, ensuring the trustworthiness and reliability of AI-generated information.



This course item explains a scenario involving malware used for cryptocurrency mining through a botnet, illustrating how malware can exploit multiple systems without owners' knowledge.

Malware and Botnets:

A bad actor wants to mine cryptocurrency efficiently by using multiple infected systems (botnet) rather than buying many machines.
Botnets consist of multiple infected computers controlled remotely to perform tasks like crypto mining without the owners' awareness.

Infection Method and Targeting:

The attacker targets gamers, specifically engineering students with good GPUs, by sending malware hidden in seemingly legitimate PDF files.
Social engineering is used by impersonating professors and sending emails with the infected PDFs disguised as free textbooks.

Detection and Consequences:

Malware protection software can detect and remove the botnet malware, especially when scanning files before download.
Victims reporting the malware leads to the botnet's decline, and a knowledgeable student eventually tracks down the attacker.

Security Advice:

Keep devices updated to protect against known vulnerabilities.
Use malware protection software to detect and prevent infections.
Avoid downloading and executing files from unknown or untrusted sources.



This course content focuses on understanding different types of malware, which are malicious software designed to harm or gain unauthorized access to computer systems.

Common Malware Categories:

Adware displays unwanted ads on devices, while malvertising uses malicious ads to deliver malware.
Keyloggers record keystrokes to capture sensitive data, and logic bombs activate malicious code upon specific triggers.

Malware with Network and System Control:

Bots automate malicious tasks and form botnets controlled remotely.
Rootkits stealthily gain unauthorized system control, and spyware covertly collects user information.

Self-Propagating and Deceptive Malware:

Viruses attach to files and spread, corrupting data and performance.
Worms spread autonomously without user action.
Trojans disguise as legitimate software to deceive users into executing harmful code.

Other Notable Malware Types:

Fileless malware operates in memory without leaving file traces.
Ransomware encrypts files and demands payment for their release.



This course item focuses on safeguarding against malware attacks by defining malware, explaining prevention methods, and outlining organizational defense strategies.

Understanding Malware:

Malware is malicious software designed to disrupt systems or gain unauthorized access, including types like ransomware, viruses, trojans, spyware, and worms.
It poses a persistent threat by stealing data, disrupting operations, and compromising security.

Preventive Measures:

Keep operating systems and software updated to close security vulnerabilities through patches.
Use reputable antivirus and antimalware software that provides real-time protection, heuristic analysis, and regular updates to detect and block threats.

User and Organizational Practices:

Create strong, unique passwords and implement multi-factor authentication to enhance account security.
Exercise caution with email attachments and downloads by verifying sources and using email filters or gateways.
Regularly back up data to enable recovery from attacks like ransomware.
Educate users on recognizing malware signs and cybersecurity best practices to reduce accidental infections.



This content explains the concept of rootkits, how hackers use them, and how security professionals detect them using a tool called rkhunter.

What is a Rootkit?

A rootkit is malicious software that hackers use to gain administrator-level access to a system.
It allows hackers to stealthily manipulate system settings and hide other malware activities from detection.

What is rkhunter and How It Works:

Rkhunter is a tool designed to detect rootkits, backdoors, and other malware on Linux/Unix systems.
It scans critical system files, compares them to known malware signatures, checks system commands, and audits configurations to find suspicious activity.

Maximizing Security with rkhunter:

Rkhunter can run on demand and alert administrators about unusual system behavior.
For best protection, it should be used alongside other security measures like intrusion detection systems, firewalls, software updates, and access controls.
